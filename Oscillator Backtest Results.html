Analysis

Montauk 8.2 dominates on risk-adjusted return. At $94.06 per dollar with a 0.51 MAR ratio, it nearly matches buy-and-hold's raw return (70% of $134.56) while cutting max drawdown from ~90%+ to 65%. The multi-exit stack (Quick EMA drives 62% of exits, ATR 31%) catches both slow rollovers and shock drops. The worst single trade is just -24.4% versus -41.2% for the oscillator.
Why 8.2 beats the oscillator strategies by 4–22x. The Montauk strategy's EMA-crossover entries (15 > 30) with trend slope confirmation are more precise than a single -0.5 composite threshold. Critically, 8.2's exits are layered and calibrated — the Quick EMA exit catches momentum collapses early (avg trade return for Quick EMA exits: +31.6%), while ATR catches crashes. The oscillator has only one exit signal, so it either exits too late (Variant A) or too early (Variant B).
The oscillator still has value — as confirmation, not as a standalone system. The -0.5 crossover entry timing largely agrees with 8.2's entries (similar trade dates), but the oscillator's single-threshold exit can't compete with 8.2's prioritized exit stack. Consider using the oscillator as an additional entry gate (suppress buys when composite is below -0.5) rather than as the primary entry/exit driver.
Variant B's steep-slope exit remains the weakest approach. It chops big winners into small ones — the 2012–2015 bull becomes +11.6% instead of 8.2's +155.0%, and the 2016–2018 run is +4.8% vs +175.5%. The slope trigger fires on healthy pullbacks, not just regime changes.
